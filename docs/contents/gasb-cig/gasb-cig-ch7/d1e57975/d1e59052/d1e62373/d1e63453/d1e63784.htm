Chapter 7, Question 7.24.11

7.24.11.Q—A state legislature passes a law to "earmark" a percentage of the state's sales tax revenues for a specific purpose. Does this action constitute enabling legislation that would restrict components of net position in the state's financial statements?
A—No. "Earmarking" an existing revenue is not equivalent to enabling legislation. The state legislature's action constitutes a commitment rather than a restriction for financial reporting purposes. The limitations can be removed by an equivalent action of the legislature.
The enabling legislation part of the definition in paragraph 34 of Statement 34, as amended, covers situations when the government passes a law that gives it the ability to levy a tax or otherwise raise revenues, and in that law, the government states that it will use those resources for a particular purpose. That arrangement is tantamount to a legally binding agreement between the government and the resource providers (the taxpayers) establishing limitations on how those funds can be used. The government generally cannot unilaterally decide to do something else with those resources. Furthermore, the government can be compelled by an external party—such as citizens, public interest groups, or the judiciary—to use the resources only for the purposes specified by the enabling legislation. (See Question 7.24.12 about subsequent amendments to enabling legislation.) This is different from situations when a government passes a law that says the use of existing resources is limited to a specific purpose or "earmarks" a portion of an existing revenue source. In those two situations the government does not obtain funds under restrictive conditions; thus, the limitations imposed do not create restrictions. (See Questions 7.35.2–7.35.4 about reporting restricted general revenues.)
