Chapter 7, Question 7.24.15

7.24.15.Q—A government, through the application of professional judgment, has always determined that it does not have legally enforceable restrictions on resource use imposed by enabling legislation. Should this government always conclude that limitations imposed by its own legislation are not legally enforceable and therefore do not constitute a restriction?
A—Prior determinations of nonenforceability should not necessarily lead a government to conclude that all enabling legislation restrictions are unenforceable. The pattern of facts underlying a government's prior determinations of nonenforceability may be relevant to future determinations. However, paragraph 3 of Statement 46 cautions that such determinations should be based on the facts and circumstances surrounding each individual restriction.
