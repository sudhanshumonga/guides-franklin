Chapter Z, Question Z.42.8

Z.42.8.Q—A school district decides to construct a new school in a rapidly developing area. Enrollment is initially projected at 1,000 students and is expected to grow to 2,500 students in 5 to 7 years. The planning, development, and construction occur over a four-year period. The school opens with only a 600-student enrollment and 5 years later reaches an enrollment of only 800 students. The less-than-expected enrollment resulted from:
a.Development in the area around the school including more commercial development and less residential development than originally projected
b.Expansion of private/nonpublic education institutions as a result of the recent availability of public education vouchers for private/nonpublic education institutions.
Should the school be evaluated for impairment? Should the situation be considered a temporary impairment?
A—Lower than projected enrollment at a new school does not indicate impairment. Paragraph 10 of Statement 42 states that a change in demand for the services of a capital asset is not considered an indicator of impairment. Change in demand may be a change from prior levels of demand or projected levels of demand for a service, as in this school example. Because the situation does not present an indicator of impairment and is therefore not an impairment, further assessment as to whether this situation would be considered a temporary impairment is not necessary. Furthermore, if the facts of the situation were different and included an indicator of impairment such as management closing a wing of the school as in Question Z.42.9, and if the subsequent test of impairment revealed the asset was impaired, the impairment would not be considered a temporary impairment because no evidence regarding reversal of the present situation was available.
