Chapter 10, Question 10.3.3

10.3.3.Q—Some governments enter into contracts to purchase U.S. debt securities that do not yet exist—“when-issued” and “to-be-announced” securities. Are these contracts derivative instruments and within the scope of Statement 53?
A—No. The primary consideration to determine if these contracts are derivative instruments and within the scope of Statement 53 should be based on whether the when-issued or to-be-announced debt security has already been reported in full as an investment based on trade-date accounting. As the security should have been recognized in full as an investment when purchased, the security does not meet the definition of a derivative instrument because there is no leverage. For example, U.S. government treasury securities and agencies may be purchased on a when-issued basis. The obligation to purchase the securities includes specific identification of the security in the form of a Committee on Uniform Security Identification Procedures number, price, date of issue, and other significant terms. That security should be recognized as an investment at its full amount with a corresponding payable. There would be no derivative instrument.
